Nobody budgets for a lost key, and the surprise is rarely the key itself. It's finding out what that little piece of plastic actually is.

Here's how the dealership and locksmith options really compare, and what makes one key cost more than another. We won't quote prices here, because the honest number depends entirely on your vehicle, but we'll show you exactly what moves it.

## What a Car Key Is Now

If your car is newer than the mid-1990s, the key is more than cut metal. Most carry a transponder chip that must be programmed to your car before the engine will start. Remotes and flip keys add buttons and a circuit board. Push-to-start fobs are small computers that talk to the vehicle constantly. That's the single biggest thing that sets the price: you're not buying a key, you're buying a key, its electronics, and the programming session that marries it to your car.

## The Dealership Route

Dealerships do this work, and for a handful of very new or unusual models they're the only option. The trade-offs are the ones you'd guess. The car usually has to come to them, which means a tow if you have no working key. Parts often get ordered rather than stocked. And you wait, sometimes days, for a slot in the service department.

Where dealers make sense: brand-new models in their first year or two, some European makes with locked-down security, and warranty situations where the key work is covered.

## The Mobile Locksmith Route

A [mobile automotive locksmith](/automotive-locksmith) comes to the car, cuts the key on-site, and programs it there. No tow, no service appointment, and for most makes and models on the road, same-day. We stock keys, remotes, and fobs for the vast majority of vehicles around Hot Springs, and when we quote, the quote includes cutting and programming, not a key that still needs a dealer visit to work.

The other thing a locksmith does that a parts counter can't: when your only key is gone, we can generate a new one from the vehicle itself. That "lost the last key" situation is our normal Tuesday.

## What Actually Moves the Price

Whoever you call, these are the levers:

**The kind of key.** Plain metal key, transponder key, remote-head key, flip key, push-to-start fob, in rising order. The more electronics, the more it costs.

**Whether it's a spare or a last-key-lost job.** Copying a working key is straightforward. Recreating a key when none exists means more time and different equipment, so it costs more. This is the whole argument for [getting a spare made](/car-key-replacement) before you need it.

**The vehicle itself.** Some brands use pricier chips and locked-down programming. A common pickup is usually simpler than a European sedan.

**Where and when.** A scheduled visit at your house beats an emergency call at a boat ramp at 10 p.m. Planned always beats stranded.
