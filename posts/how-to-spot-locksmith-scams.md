After 15+ years working Hot Springs, we know every legitimate operator in this market, and we know the playbook the fakes run. Here it is, so it doesn't run on you.

## How the Scam Works

The setup is a call center, usually out of state, buying ads and fake listings for every town in America. The listing shows a local-looking address and a very low price for a [car opening](/automotive-locksmith) or house lockout. You call, a dispatcher quotes the low price, and a subcontractor is sent your way.

Then the price changes. The tech looks at your ordinary lock and declares it "high security" or "impossible to pick." The advertised price becomes a service fee, and the real bill lands at many times the quote. On house calls, the favorite move is drilling a lock that never needed drilling, then charging you for a cheap replacement lock on top. Payment is demanded in cash or through an app, and the "company" behind it can't be found the next day.

## The Red Flags, in Order

**A price that sounds like bait.** A professional cannot drive to you, open your car without damage, and stay in business at the price those ads show. If a quote sounds impossible, it is. It's the hook, not the price.

**No straight answer on the phone.** Ask the company's name and where they're actually based. Scam dispatchers answer with "locksmith service" and dodge the location question, because they're answering for a hundred towns at once.

**An unmarked car and no ID.** Legitimate locksmiths show up in a marked vehicle or with company ID, and in Arkansas you can ask to see credentials. A tech who arrives in a plain sedan with a drill and no paperwork is telling you everything.

**Drilling as the first move.** Real locksmiths pick, decode, or bypass locks nondestructively almost every time. Drilling is a last resort for damaged or failed locks, not the opening act on a standard deadbolt. If the drill comes out in the first five minutes, stop the job.

**Cash only, price rising.** The quote should survive the drive to your house. Ours does.

## How to Check Who You're Calling

Two minutes of checking beats an hour of regret:

- Look at the reviews, and look at where they are. A real local company has [reviews from real neighbors](/customer-reviews) going back years, not a burst of five-star reviews posted the same month.
- Match the phone number across the website, the Google listing, and the ad. Scam networks rotate numbers constantly.
- Ask something local. Where are you based? How long to get to the Gorge Road area? A Hot Springs locksmith answers like someone who drives these roads every day, because we do.
- Get the price for your specific situation on the phone, and get what would change it. A pro can tell you the honest variables before rolling a truck.
