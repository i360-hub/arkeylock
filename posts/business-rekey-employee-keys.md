Here's a question every business owner should be able to answer and almost none can: how many keys to your building exist, and who has them? If your answer starts with "probably," this article is for you.

We've done commercial lock work around Hot Springs for more than 15 years, for restaurants on Central, shops downtown, offices, churches, and warehouses out on the highway. The call always comes the same way: someone left, the key didn't come back, and now the owner is lying awake doing math about who else has copies. Here's how to handle it, and how to stop having to.

## The Key Didn't Come Back. Now What?

Be honest about the risk without being dramatic. Most departed employees never think about that key again. The problem isn't the person, it's the copies. That key may have been copied at a hardware store, loaned out, or left in a car that got broken into. Once a key leaves your control, you can't answer the question "who can get in?" And a building full of your inventory, records, and registers deserves a better answer than "not sure."

The fix is the same as a house: a [rekey](/rekey-vs-replace-locks). Your locks stay on the doors, the insides change, and every old key stops working at once. For most storefronts and offices it's a quick visit. After it, the answer to "who has copies?" is simple: nobody.

When the departure was hostile, or the missing keys open something sensitive, treat it as same-day work. That's a call we take [around the clock](/emergency-locksmith), not a next-week appointment.

## Stop Rekeying Every Time: The Master Key Fix

If you're rekeying more than once a year, the real problem is how your keys are set up. A master key system fixes it. Each employee gets a key that opens only what their job needs, like the front door and their work area. You carry one master that opens everything. When a worker's key walks off, we rekey that level only, not the whole building. Your office, stockroom, and server closet were never exposed at all.

We set these up for [businesses](/commercial-locksmith) of every size around Hot Springs. The pattern is always the same. Owners think master keys are for hospitals. Then they find out it fits any building with more than two doors and more than three employees.

## Or Skip Physical Keys Where It Hurts Most

For the doors with the most staff traffic, keypads and access control beat metal keys outright. A code gets deleted in thirty seconds the day someone leaves. No locksmith visit at all. Electronic doors also log who came in and when, and that log has settled more than one inventory dispute.

The setup that works for a lot of local businesses is a mix. Keypads on the main doors where staff come and go. Master-keyed locks on the inside doors. And for anything critical, high-security keys that can't be copied at a kiosk. That last part matters, because "do not duplicate" stamped on a normal key is a suggestion, not a lock.

## The Turnover Checklist

Make key control part of offboarding, the same as the email password:

- Collect keys on the last day. Log which keys each person gets on their first day, so there's something to check against
- Rekey when a key doesn't come back, when someone had after-hours access, or when the parting was rough
- Delete codes and fobs the same day for electronic doors
- Once a year, count the keys against the log, and rekey if the math doesn't work

None of this is paranoia. You already track your cash and your passwords. The doors deserve the same habit.
