Half the fob calls we take don't end with us selling a fob. That's not great business advice, but it's the truth: a lot of "my key fob died" problems are a battery, a wet weekend at the lake, or a fob that just needs to be reprogrammed. After 15 years of these calls around Hot Springs, here's the checklist we'd run on our own trucks, cheapest fix first.

## Start With the Battery, Really

It sounds too simple, and it's the answer more often than everything else combined. Fob batteries fade slowly, so the first symptom is usually range. You used to unlock the car from the porch, now you have to stand next to it. Then the buttons get inconsistent, then they quit.

Most fobs take a common coin battery that costs a few dollars at any store in town, and most fobs open with a small screwdriver at the seam. Look for the battery type stamped inside the cover, swap it, and test. If you'd rather not pry at it, bring it by when we're in your area and it's a two-minute job.

## Car Says "Key Not Detected"? Try the Backup Spot

Push-to-start cars have a hidden trick almost nobody knows until they need it. When the fob battery is too weak for the car to sense it, there's a backup location, often the cupholder, a marked spot on the console, or holding the fob against the start button itself. The car reads the chip directly at that spot, and it will start with a completely dead fob battery. Your owner's manual shows where. That trick has gotten a lot of people home from Walmart.

There's usually a hidden metal key in the fob too, for the door. Slide the little release on the fob and it comes out. Many door handles hide the keyhole under a plastic cap.

## One Button Works, Another Doesn't

That's not the battery. Buttons wear out individually from use, and lock buttons go first because they get pressed the most. Sometimes the rubber pad fails, sometimes the contact under it. A locksmith can often repair the shell or swap the electronics into a new case, which beats buying a whole new fob when the brains still work.

## The Fob Went in the Lake

A Hot Springs classic. Fresh water isn't an instant death sentence for a fob if you act fast. Get the battery out immediately, don't press buttons, and let it dry completely for a day or two, opened up, before testing. No rice necessary, just air and patience. Lake Hamilton dunks that get the battery out fast survive more often than you'd think. Saltwater or a full day underwater is usually the end, and then you're shopping for a [replacement](/car-key-replacement). Boat keys and dock box locks take the same abuse, and we handle [marine work](/rv-marine-locksmith) too.

## Nothing Works, Battery Is New

Now it's one of three things. The fob lost its programming, which can happen after a dead car battery or a jump start. The fob's electronics failed. Or, less often, the problem is on the car's side, a bad antenna or receiver. The first two are locksmith work: we can reprogram your existing fob or program a new one at your location, and we can test which one you actually need instead of guessing. If it's the car's receiver, we'll tell you that too, because a new fob won't fix it and we don't sell parts that won't fix things.

## The Two-Fob Rule

Here's the habit that saves people money: keep two working fobs, always. When you're down to one, every problem becomes urgent and expensive, and if that last fob is lost, generating keys from scratch is a bigger job than copying one. The day you notice you're down to a single working key is the day to [get a spare programmed](/automotive-locksmith), on your schedule, at your house, not in a parking lot after hours.
